üß™ The EPR Paradox

In 1935, Einstein, Podolsky, and Rosen published a famous paper arguing that quantum mechanics must be incomplete. They described entangled particles ‚Äî pairs that share a quantum state such that measuring one instantly determines the state of the other, regardless of distance. Einstein called this "spooky action at a distance" and believed it proved quantum mechanics was missing some "hidden variables."

"I cannot seriously believe in [quantum theory] because... physics should represent a reality in time and space, free from spooky actions at a distance."

‚Äî Albert Einstein, 1947

üîî Bell's Theorem & Bell Tests

In 1964, physicist John Bell devised a mathematical inequality that could distinguish between Einstein's "hidden variables" and true quantum entanglement. Decades of experiments ‚Äî culminating in the 2022 Nobel Prize-winning work ‚Äî have consistently violated Bell's inequality, proving that quantum entanglement is real and that Einstein's local hidden variables cannot explain nature.
